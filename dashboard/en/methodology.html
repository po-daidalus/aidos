Methodology

How aidos surveys, computes and publishes

Every figure on this site must be traceable to a publicly visible statement. This page explains where the data comes from, what we compute from it — and what we deliberately do not do.

1. The data source

The basis is the Google Maps transparency notice: since 2026, Google states on public business profiles how many reviews were removed in the past 365 days “due to complaints about defamation”. aidos records exclusively what is visible to everyone there:

  • the removal notice with its range (e.g. “51 to 100”, at most “over 250”),
  • the currently displayed average rating and the star distribution,
  • the number of visible reviews, industry and location.

Not recorded: the content or authors of individual reviews — neither visible nor removed ones.

2. The survey

  1. Candidates: business profiles are systematically compiled from open sources (incl. OpenStreetMap) — city by city, across industries.
  2. Checking: every profile is checked for the transparency notice. Profiles without a notice are counted too — that is the only way to say credibly how widespread removals really are (a measured denominator instead of anecdote).
  3. Monthly repetition: the survey re-runs monthly. Every entry carries a survey date; entries that are not re-confirmed disappear from the public view.

On reach: the notice is rare — in the cities surveyed so far, only around 3–5 % of checked profiles carry it. Since July 2026 the survey logs every checked URL with its outcome; the resulting measured rate per city is shown in the data-basis overview.

3. What we compute

Ranges, never false precision

Google publishes ranges, not exact figures. We compute with these ranges and report them as such. For “over 250” the true number is unknown — we cap conservatively and label the dark figure.

The “rating without removals” estimate

For sufficiently documented profiles we estimate where the rating would stand if the removed reviews still counted: Google's published range is arithmetically re-inserted into the real star distribution at 1–2★ (best/worst case yields the range). This is explicitly an estimate — if the removals were justified (e.g. fake campaigns), the displayed rating is the more accurate one.

The aidos score

The score (0–100) is a percentile: it ranks a profile by its number of removed reviews within the collected dataset. A score of 90 means “more removals than 90 % of surveyed profiles” — a neutral statistical classification, not a value judgement and no proof of misconduct.

4. Whom we name — and whom we don't

Names are published only for legal entities and chains. Only corporations (GmbH, AG …), larger chains and high-revenue businesses appear publicly by name. Sole traders and micro businesses enter the statistics only anonymised in aggregates (industry, city, count) and are stored pseudonymised internally. Details in the privacy policy (DE).

5. Limits of the data

  • The removal notice is a rolling 365-day total. When individual reviews were removed is not published by Google — which is why we show no removal curves, only verifiable trajectories and clearly labelled model calculations.
  • All values are snapshots of the public Google data at the respective survey date.
  • Who requested a removal is not publicly visible. We therefore write “Google removed after defamation complaints” — never that a business “had reviews deleted”.
  • A high number of removed reviews is no evidence of unfair conduct: businesses are frequently targeted by unjustified fake campaigns; removals can be entirely legitimate.

6. Corrections

Mistakes happen — and get corrected. Affected businesses can contest their data via report / correct data (DE) or contact@aidos.tech. If a review is not completed within 5 days, we precautionarily remove the entry from public view. Substantive corrections are noted on the affected page.
